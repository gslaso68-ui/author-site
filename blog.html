Confusion in the Wild

A recurring series examining how strategic ambiguity, narrative control, and gray-zone tactics shape current geopolitics, through the lens of The Art of Confusion.

Each entry follows the same structure: The Event → The Confusion Mechanism → Who Benefits (and How).

In each case, confusion is not a failure of strategy — it is the strategy.

Confusion in the Wild #1: Consensus Without Commitment (G20 Johannesburg)

Theme: multilateral ambiguity · signaling · strategic flexibility
The Event: World leaders gathered at the G20 Summit in Johannesburg amid heightened geopolitical and economic strain, yet the meeting concluded without firm consensus on major security or financial coordination issues. Attendance by senior leaders was uneven, and final communiqués remained deliberately broad.
Source: G20 Johannesburg Summit reporting, November 2025
The Confusion Mechanism: Ambiguous language and non-committal outcomes obscure where leadership truly lies, allowing competing interpretations of unity, division, and intent.
Who Benefits — and How: Major powers benefit by avoiding binding obligations while maintaining the appearance of engagement. Confusion preserves strategic flexibility, enabling bilateral maneuvering behind a multilateral façade.
ambiguity strategic flexibility signaling optional commitment

Confusion in the Wild #2: Partnership Rhetoric, Strategic Ambiguity (EU–AU Summit)

Theme: alignment hedging · leverage · narrative unity
The Event: The European Union and African Union met in Luanda to reaffirm cooperation on development, security, and trade, while avoiding explicit alignment choices amid growing global power competition.
Source: EU–African Union Summit briefings, November 2025
The Confusion Mechanism: Public emphasis on shared values masks unresolved tensions over influence, investment, and alignment, leaving external observers uncertain about long-term strategic direction.
Who Benefits — and How: Both sides benefit by keeping options open. Strategic ambiguity reduces immediate friction while preserving leverage with competing global partners, particularly China and the United States.
hedging leverage alignment narrative control

Confusion in the Wild #3: Domestic Turbulence as External Signal (Greece)

Theme: uncertainty pressure · negotiation · policy hesitation
The Event: Widespread farmer protests across Greece disrupted transport routes, ports, and borders, while government messaging alternated between conciliation and firmness, offering no clear resolution timeline.
Source: European media and EU monitoring reports, December 2025
The Confusion Mechanism: Shifting signals blur the line between temporary unrest and structural instability, complicating assessments by markets, investors, and European institutions.
Who Benefits — and How: Ambiguity increases negotiating pressure. By allowing uncertainty to linger, domestic actors extract attention and concessions while external stakeholders hesitate to impose conditions or respond decisively.
delay pressure signal fog leverage

Confusion in the Wild #4: Force Without Declaration (China–Taiwan Drills)

Theme: coercive signaling · threshold management · ambiguity
The Event: China conducted large-scale “Justice Mission 2025” military exercises around Taiwan, disrupting civilian air traffic and maritime activity without issuing explicit escalation threats.
Source: Regional security reporting, December 2025
The Confusion Mechanism: Military signaling is deliberately decoupled from political intent, making it unclear whether the action represents rehearsal, deterrence, or coercive messaging.
Who Benefits — and How: This benefits the initiator by sustaining pressure while avoiding red lines. Confusion forces neighbors and partners to prepare for multiple scenarios simultaneously, diluting response focus and increasing psychological strain.
threshold control deterrence coercion optional escalation

Confusion in the Wild #5: Oscillation as Leverage (Yemen & the Red Sea)

Theme: mixed signaling · deniability · maneuver space
The Event: Houthi forces alternated between attacks on regional targets, prisoner releases, and diplomatic signaling throughout late November and December, creating mixed interpretations of intent.
Source: UN briefings and regional conflict reporting, November–December 2025
The Confusion Mechanism: Contradictory actions blur the boundary between escalation and de-escalation, making it difficult to determine whether behavior signals moderation or tactical repositioning.
Who Benefits — and How: The actor gains room to maneuver. Confusion delays decisive external response, fragments international consensus, and allows coercion and diplomacy to be played simultaneously.
mixed signals deniability tempo maneuver space
